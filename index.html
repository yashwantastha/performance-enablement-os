What if performance reviews worked differently?

A working prototype exploring what modern performance management could look like — built after researching how Adobe, Microsoft, Google, Netflix, Deloitte, Accenture, and others have redesigned theirs. Seven modules, each addressing one structural problem with how reviews are done today. Use the tabs to walk through them. Each tab opens with a plain-language explainer.

1 · Evidence Pack
💡 In plain language
Today, your manager writes your review from memory — and forgets most of the year. With this, AI gathers proof from the systems you already work in (Jira, Confluence, Slack, CSAT, calendar) and drafts a one-page evidence summary. Your manager edits and signs it. You see the same evidence — no surprises, no recency bias, no "I forgot what you did in February".
Outcomes aggregation, not activity monitoring. Cuts manager prep ~70% (Adobe data). The report gets something concrete to challenge or build on, instead of a manager's hazy summary judgement.

⚖️ EU AI Act compliance — addressed by design

AI used in performance evaluation is classified high-risk under EU AI Act Annex III (employment). This system is designed for compliance from day one: Claude only drafts from observable artifacts (no opaque scoring), the manager owns and signs every decision, the report sees the same evidence (transparency), and a DPIA + human-oversight log is built into the workflow. EU HQ = highest scrutiny — we lean into it as a feature, not a risk.

2 · Two Loops — separate coaching from compensation
💡 In plain language
Today, one annual conversation tries to do two things that fight each other: "help you grow" and "decide your salary." When money is on the line, no one says the hard truth — managers soften, reports defend. So we split them. A monthly growth chat (low stakes, honest, forward-looking) and a separate annual decision moment (high stakes, evidenced, transparent).
Adobe did this in 2012 — voluntary attrition fell ~30% in year one. Accenture rolled it out for 330,000 employees in 2015. Same playbook, applied at our scale.

🌀 Coaching Loop — "performance enablement"

Continuous · monthly cadence · low stakes
  • Auto-pulse: 2-question check-in to manager + report
  • Claude synthesises last month's evidence + Slack themes into a 1-page coaching note
  • 30-min 1:1 around it — focus on growth, not rating
  • Development Focus refreshed quarterly (3 items max)
  • No comp implications · no calibration language
  • Public to skip-level: "are growth conversations actually happening?"

⚖️ Calibration Loop — annual, structured, evidenced

Annual · transparent · high stakes
  • Aggregates the year's evidence packs + project review ratings
  • Calibration session: managers ranked on evidence quality, not opinions
  • LLM flags pattern bias before discussion (e.g. "Manager X consistently rates [demo group] 0.4 lower")
  • Comp + promotion decisions made here and only here
  • Outcome: a 1-page snapshot, signed by report
  • Surprises forbidden — every datapoint familiar from coaching loop

3 · Project Reviews roll up to a Person Rating
💡 In plain language
Today, your year is judged by your manager's overall impression — one person, one moment, lots of bias. With this, every project you finish gets rated by the people who actually worked with you on it — right after delivery, while memory is fresh. Your year-end rating is the average. Many honest snapshots beat one summary judgement, and it's much harder to game with politics.
Deloitte's 4-question model (HBR Apr 2015) cut their PM burden by ~2 million hours/year. Stripe runs a peer-weighted version. Eight to twelve datapoints per person per year — far richer than one manager + four hand-picked 360 reviewers.

4 · Manager Scorecard — managers get reviewed too
💡 In plain language
Today, only employees get reviewed. But who reviews managers? With this, every manager has their own scorecard: did your team grow? Did anyone leave who shouldn't have? Are growth conversations actually happening? Their boss sees the same scorecard — and it cascades all the way to the C-suite. You can't be a "great manager" if your people aren't growing.
Replaces the theatre of anonymous upward feedback with measurable team-health outcomes. Gartner: manager capability (measured by team outcomes, not training hours) is the #1 differentiator between PM systems that lift performance and ones that don't.

6 · Internal Talent Marketplace
💡 In plain language
Today, when a role opens up, we usually hire externally — expensive, slow, and our own people don't grow. With this, the system already knows everyone's strengths (because the Evidence Pack has been collecting them) and recommends internal candidates automatically. Cheaper than external hires, your people grow without leaving, and the new role gets filled from someone who already knows the company. People grow by moving sideways, not just by leaving.
Plugs into existing skill-based mobility ambitions. Every evidence pack and project rating feeds the marketplace automatically — no separate skills survey, no LinkedIn-style profile to maintain. Integrates with Workday as the system of record for org structure, role definitions, and headcount approvals — internal candidates surface before an external req is even opened. In a headcount-constrained environment (e.g. cost reduction targets), this is the mechanism that keeps high performers engaged and growing without adding headcount.

7 · Early Warning System — for trouble and for growth
💡 In plain language
Today, performance issues get discovered at year-end as nasty surprises. Same with people who are ready for a promotion — managers miss them. With this, the system watches signals all year and flags both: "this person is in trouble" (red) and "this person is ready for the next level" (green) — early enough to actually do something. No more surprise PIPs. No more missed promotions.
Symmetrical by design — catches risk and growth. Triggers structured conversations when signals cross thresholds, so action is consistent across managers instead of dependent on individual vigilance.

📚 What leading companies actually tried — and what worked
💡 In plain language
These ideas aren't theoretical. Adobe, Microsoft, Google, Netflix, Deloitte, Accenture, GE, and Stripe have all redesigned their performance reviews over the past 10–15 years. Some succeeded (Adobe, Accenture). Some made things worse (Google's GRAD, GE's removal of all differentiation). The patterns are clear — this panel shows what worked, what backfired, and what to avoid.
Receipts with sources. Where a number is cited, the link is verifiable. The case studies are also a defence against the "we don't know what would happen" objection — others have run this experiment.
Adobe
Check-In · 2012
What changed
Killed annual reviews + stack ranking. Quarterly forward-looking "Check-Ins". Comp decoupled.
Result
Voluntary attrition –30%; involuntary attrition +50% (faster action on low performers); ~80,000 manager hours saved/year.
"We were spending 80,000 hours a year on something nobody liked." — Donna Morris, SVP HR
Microsoft
Connect · 2013, deepened by Nadella
What changed
Killed forced distribution / "the curve". Replaced with frequent "Connects" focused on impact + contribution to others' success.
Result
Cultural shift away from talent hoarding; Glassdoor / engagement scores climbed sharply; "growth mindset" became a corporate operating principle.
"We need to move from a know-it-all to a learn-it-all culture." — Satya Nadella
Google
GRAD · 2022
What changed
Cut from 2x/year to annual. New 5-tier rating, more "Significant Impact" by design. More pay differentiation at top.
What backfired
Comms. Employees read GRAD as cost-cutting in disguise — coincided with budget tightening. Sundar held an all-hands to address backlash.
Lesson for DH: never roll out PM redesign in the same quarter as a comp freeze.
Netflix
No formal reviews
What changed
Continuous informal 360 ("start/stop/continue"). Annual "keeper test". Generous severance instead of PIPs.
What backfired
Reported fear culture (NYT 2018). Not transferable to 50k employees — assumes only senior, autonomous talent. Cherry-pick the principles, not the model.
Useful for: candor norms, severance-not-PIP for clear misfits.
Deloitte
4-Question Model · 2015
What changed
Killed cascading goals + ratings + 360s. Project leaders answer 4 forward-looking questions per person, per project.
Result
Saved ~2 million hours/year firm-wide. Real-time signal replaces historical record.
"We're after real-time insight into performance, not historical data." — Buckingham & Goodall, HBR
Accenture
Performance Achievement · 2015
What changed
Eliminated annual reviews + ratings + forced ranking for 330,000 employees. Continuous app-based feedback.
Result
Estimated multi-hundred-million-dollar manager-time saving. Pay differentiation rebuilt iteratively over 3 cycles.
"If you believe you selected the right person, give them freedom to innovate." — Pierre Nanterme, CEO
GE
PD@GE · 2016 → quietly reversed 2018
What changed
Killed Welch-era 20-70-10 stack ranking. App-based continuous "insights/considerations".
What backfired
Removed ratings and differentiation. Top performers felt invisible. Larry Culp quietly reintroduced ratings in 2018.
The lesson: keep differentiation, kill the ranking ceremony.
Stripe
Project-based + peer-weighted
What works
Bi-annual cycles aligned to project arcs. 4–6 jointly-chosen peer reviewers per person. Ratings exist but are de-emphasised in conversation.
Watch-out
Peer-review tax — engineers report writing 6+ reviews each cycle. Cap reviewer requests per person.
Works because peers see engineering work better than managers do. Same logic for IT/Ops.
Industry trend
Forced ranking is dead, differentiation isn't
Data
Fortune 500 using forced ranking: 49% (2009) → ~14% (2018). But: GE, Google (GRAD tiers), Amazon (URA), Meta have all quietly re-added cohort identification.
Implication
The honest framing: don't promise "no differentiation." Promise fair, evidenced, transparent differentiation instead.
Source: CEB / Gartner (verify before pitch)
Common failure modes to acknowledge in the meeting: (1) Removing ratings without replacing differentiation (GE); (2) Coupling redesign with comp cuts (Google); (3) Under-investing in manager capability — the #1 cause of redesign failure; (4) Treating it as an HR project, not a business transformation; (5) Free-text bias — gendered/personality-focused feedback for underrepresented groups (Stanford VMware Women's Lab, 2014).
A working prototype, not a finished proposal
Built to explore what's possible based on research from companies that have already redesigned their reviews. Each of the seven modules can be piloted independently. The AI layer means we don't need a 12-month rollout to learn what works — a single team can test a module in a quarter and deprioritise it if it doesn't move the needle.
